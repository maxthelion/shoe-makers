# Insight: Annealing Schedule for Reactive/Proactive Balance

## Lens

**Simulated Annealing** — an optimization algorithm inspired by metallurgy. In annealing, metal is heated to high temperature (random exploration) then slowly cooled (exploitation of best-found state). The cooling schedule controls the transition: too fast and you get stuck in local minima; too slow and you waste time.

## Connection

The shoe-makers system has a natural temperature analogue: the **reactive ratio** detected by `shift-summary.ts`. A high reactive ratio means the system is "hot" — spending energy fixing things, bouncing between critiques and fixes. A low reactive ratio means the system is "cool" — steadily executing proactive work.

Currently this ratio is observed but not acted on. The `buildProcessSuggestions()` function surfaces warnings when reactive ratio > 70%, but nothing in the system adapts its behaviour in response. The system has a thermometer but no thermostat.

## Proposal

Introduce an **annealing-aware explore prompt**. When the explore elf runs, it already receives invariant counts and health scores. Add the **recent reactive ratio** (from the last shift summary or last N ticks in the current shift log) to its context. The explore prompt would then shift its candidate generation strategy:

- **High temperature** (reactive ratio > 60%): candidates should focus on root causes — "why are tests breaking?" not "fix this test". Look for architectural issues, missing test infrastructure, flaky patterns. The goal is to cool the system down by fixing the source of churn, not the symptoms.
- **Low temperature** (reactive ratio < 30%): candidates can be more ambitious — new features, creative improvements, spec extensions. The system is stable enough to take risks.
- **Medium temperature**: mixed candidates, balanced between consolidation and advancement.

This would be implemented as additional context in the explore prompt template (`src/prompts/three-phase.ts`), not as new tree nodes. The tree stays deterministic; only the prompt content adapts.

## Why

The current system treats every explore tick identically regardless of system health trends. An elf exploring after 5 consecutive critique/fix cycles should look for different things than an elf exploring after 5 clean executions. The annealing lens makes this explicit: the system's recent history should influence its exploration strategy, gradually shifting from stabilization to ambition as health improves.

This builds on the existing `processPatterns` infrastructure in `shift-summary.ts` without adding new tree nodes or violating the pure-function-agent model.
