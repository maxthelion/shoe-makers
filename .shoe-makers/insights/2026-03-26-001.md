## Lens

**Kayapinar, Kiziltepe** — A small Kurdish neighbourhood (population 495) within the larger district of Kiziltepe, itself within Mardin Province. Notably, the village has dual naming: the official Turkish name "Kayapinar" and the Kurdish name "Tahtoka Heci Seid." It's populated by a single tribe (Xalecan) — a small, cohesive unit embedded within a larger administrative hierarchy.

## Connection

The shoe-makers system has a naming/identity parallel. Each elf invocation is anonymous — there's no identity, no lineage, no tribal memory. One elf writes code, another reviews it, but neither knows who the other "is." The `last-action.md` file tells the reviewer what rules the previous elf was given, but not what the elf actually experienced, struggled with, or discovered along the way.

Kayapinar's dual naming (official Turkish vs. local Kurdish) mirrors a tension in the system: the **official name** of work (the work-item title, the commit message) vs. the **local name** — what the elf actually encountered while doing the work. Currently only the official name survives. The local knowledge dies with each invocation.

The Xalecan tribe analogy also resonates: a small cohesive group within a larger hierarchy. Each shift's sequence of elves forms a de facto "tribe" — they share context through state files, build on each other's work, and have a shared goal. But unlike a real tribe, they have no awareness of this kinship.

## Proposal

Add a **shift memoir** mechanism: a lightweight, append-only file (`.shoe-makers/state/shift-memoir.md`) where each elf can leave one or two sentences of informal context for the next elf — not the official work-item or commit message, but the "local name" of what happened:

- "The claim-evidence YAML schema is confusing — the wiki says singular file but it's actually a directory. Future elves will hit this."
- "I wanted to refactor the tree evaluator but the tests are too brittle to touch safely right now."
- "The health score for prompts.test.ts has been stuck at 87 for several shifts — might need a different approach."

This differs from findings (which are formal, structured, and reviewed) and shift logs (which are automated timestamps). The memoir is informal tribal knowledge — the Kurdish name alongside the Turkish one.

The setup script would include the last few memoir entries in the elf's prompt context, giving each invocation a sense of what the "tribe" (this shift's sequence of elves) has been experiencing.

## Why

Currently, valuable context is lost between invocations. An elf might spend time investigating something, conclude it's not worth pursuing, but leave no trace of that reasoning. The next elf (or a future shift) might waste time rediscovering the same dead end. Findings are too formal for this — you wouldn't write a finding for "I noticed X is confusing." The memoir captures the informal, experiential knowledge that makes a tribe cohesive — the kind of thing you'd tell a colleague in passing, not write in a report.
